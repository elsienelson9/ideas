ApproachToUnderstanding

29th March 2022 at 8:09am

Here, inspired by a random commercial email I got as one of the academic-support blasts, I'm going to try to write about what my own approach to "theory" is - what do I consider my tools of perception and understanding?

I suppose the starting places are ontology and epistemology, first.

In terms of the nature of existence, I do think there is a "real" world "out there." I think there "are" objects, processes, and relationships. I think these exist outside of the act of knowing them. I think if a tree falls in a forest and no one hears it, it makes a sound. I believe the sound is the sound waves, not the perception of the waves.

In terms of epistemology, I believe that every act of knowing is positioned; I believe that all acts of knowing embed culture, which embeds the good and the bad (including power, creativity, technology, mistaken assumptions); I think some positions provide better views than others ("better" referring to more comprehensive, or revealing interesting secrets, or just showing things that were heretofore unrecognized â€“ and these "better" qualities are of course specific to the context, and moment, and would likely change over time and goal); I think that no act of knowing can comprehend the entirety of its object (not only because the complexity of existence must exceed the potential of text/illustration representation - there, I guess, I have some kind of Platonic metaphor, and maybe a Derrida-ian sense that we only have access to "the text" although I think it is beyond "text"); I believe the goals of knowledge-seeking include both the pure goal of understanding for its own beauty, and the goal of identifying possibilities for improvements.

Research is a political act at several levels. The choice of topic prioritizes some things over others. That priority reflects pre-existing values. The choice of tools and methods for investigation also reflect existing systems of power and authority. Does it follow that choosing different tools and methods is an act of resistance â€“ and, if it does, is it an efficacious act of resistance â€“ and, if so, what is the concept of efficacious here? My current discipline is so gosh-darned obsessed with the politics of knowing that I swallow this whole - but I don't think, deep-down, I think the politics of knowing is the most important part. Yes, I deeply believe that making the world a better place is a central meaning-work of humanity, but that "making" of "a better place" is accomplished in a wide, wide variety of ways.

I also recognize that my own desire to "know" is motivated by "interests" in both senses of the word. First, I think my desire to know is an act of self-assertion and self-making: I like to think of myself as an active, curious person-in-the-world. It is the act of my intellectual agency. And (as Maxine Craig reminded me yesterday on a walk), we are reproducing our class privilege in all our academic pursuits. It is a privilege to be an academic, and being an academic reinforces that privilege. And secondly, in the other sense of the word, my research follows my interests - my curiosities â€“ which are of course framed in the context of my own environment. It's not that I can't see past my environment, myself, but that things strike me because of who I am. There is no objective place from which even to be a curious person.

On the Korean peninsula between June 1950 and xx 1953, a violent, internecine war, fueled by global ideological antagonisms and neo-imperial cold war ambitions, raged up and down the 1,200 kilometer expanse, affecting every Korean community. In the first wave, North Korean troops swept past the South Korean capital, Seoul, in three days, pushing millions [?] of civilians southward ahead of their advance. The Northern forces (the KPA?) reached the outskirts of South Korea's second largest city, Pusan, by early August, before a mid-September attack of UN-US forces at their western flank in Incheon. The US-UN forces, led by US General Douglas MacArthur, broke the supply lines and harried the Northern army back, ultimately all the way north to the Yalu River on the DPRK's border with China. MacArthur's strategic plan to cut off Chinese support for the KPA, combined with ambitions to unbalance the communists in China, resulted in the Chinese army joining with the DPRK troops in late October, and this combined force once again retook the northern territory and marched further, once again occupying Seoul in January of 1951. The UN forces reclaimed Seoul in mid-March, and for the next two years the war continued with little change in the siting of the front line, which was declared a demilitarized zone when the armistice was signed in the summer of 1953.

These three years of "hot war" generated xxxx casualties in north and south Korea. While the majority of dead were civilians, a significant share were members of the military.

According to Eui Hang Shin (2001), "The Korean War was among the world's most destructive wars, in proportion to the population. During the war, the population of South Korea declined by nearly two million, excluding an influx of nearly 650,000 North Korean refugees. During the same period, about 290,000 South Koreans migrated to North Korea, either by force or by choice. Redistribution of the South Korean population continued on a large scale even into the immediate post-war years." Shin notes that mortality figures for the war years are "extremely incomplete" and he warns of "inaccuracies plaguing the censuses of 1949 and 1955, including serious undercounting and age misreporting." Shin's best estimate of the death toll (citing the prior work of Kwon) is between 1.5 and 2 million war related mortalities. The losses were not distributed evenly across the population; a disproportionate share of the casualties were men between the ages of 20 and 34, and while some of this population loss was offset by the also disproportionate influx of North Korean male refugees, the overall sex ratio of the South Korean population appears to have fallen from 101.1 men for every 100 women in 1949 to 80.5 men per 100 women in 1955. Shin also discusses the effects of war-related migration and displacement, which, he argues, "distorted the relationship of individuals and families to the economic system because of the breakdown of economic establishments and the consequent softening of prewar social class structures. The rapid urbanization process created new dimensions in social stratification systems in post-war Korea." (Shin 2001: 136)

In the wake of the war, Koreans north and south of the new Demilitarized Zone faced unprecedented challenges to their most basic needs. Pointing to the disproportionate loss of young South Korean men, Shin focuses on the demographic disruptions that impacted South Korean women. Many women, young and old, were pushed to take on money-earning roles, and the US bases sometimes and in some places provided locals the only opportunities for economic activity. Corinthians

9th May 2022 at 1:38pm

In the Epistles of Paul, King James Version, First Corinthians articulates some of the first, and most extreme, rules regarding chastity for Christians. First Corinthians is of disputed authorial authenticity; it is presumed to have been written around 53 or 54 CE, addressed to the members of a Christian church located at Corinth, a settlement at the chokepoint between the mainland and the road to Athens and the cities of the Peloponesse. The author, whom we will call Paul although it may have been written in his name by someone else, introduces the topic of "fornication" (King James translation) in 1 Corinthians 5:

"It is reported commonly that there is fornication among you, and such fornication as it not named among the Gentiles, that one should have his father's wife. And ye are puffed up, and have not rather mourned, that he hath done this deed might be taken away from among you. For I verily, as absent in body, but present in spirit, have judged already, as though I were present, concerning him that hath so done this deed, In the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, when ye are gathered together, and my spirit, with the power of our Lord Jesus Christ, To deliver such an one unto Satan for the destruction of the flesh, that the spirit may be saved in the day of the Lord Jesus. Your glorying is not good. Know ye that a little leaven leaveneth the whole lump? Purge out therefore the old leaven, that ye may be a new lump, as ye are unleavened." It goes on with this metaphor for a while, encouraging the Corinthians to purge the sexual offender from their midst.

But that is just the first mention of sexuality in Corinthians. Paul goes on in 1 Corinthians 6 to name several faults of the unrighteous: "neither fornicators, nor idolaters, nor adulterers, nor effeminate, nor abusers of themselves with mankind, nor thieves, nor covetous, nor drunkards, nor revilers, nor extortioners, shall inherit the kingdom of God." He goes on, further in Corinthians 6: "Meats for the belly, and the belly for meats: but God shall destroy both it and them. Now the body is not for fornication, but for the Lord; and the Lord for the body. ...Know ye not that your bodies are the members of Christ? shall I then take the members of Christ, and make them the members of an harlot? God forbid. What? know ye not that he which is joined to an harlot is one body? for two, saith he, shall be one flesh. but he that is joined unto the Lord is one spirit. Flee fornication. Every sin that a man doeth is without the body; but he that committeth fornication sinneth against his own body. What? know ye not that your body is the temple of the Holy Ghost which is in you, which ye have of God, and ye are not your own? For ye are bought with a price: therefore glorify God in your body, and in your spirit, which are God's."

And this is all preface to the discussion of sex within marriage.

Men and Breast Cancer

5th September 2022 at 9:21am

This may serve as a draft of a chapter or article on men and breast cancer. Ideas include Jealousy Incarnate, the Pink movement, and my grandfather.

My grandfather died when I was 12 years old. Despite the fact that we had just visited my grandparents, who lived a few hours' drive away, I was surprised by the news. I was a death innocent, and hadn't read the signs: racking cough catching the end of laughter, huge watery eyes sunken into sockets, and the maudlin energy of the family milling around. I didn't even know he was ill. I love my grandfather still, more perhaps as I grow older and learn more of his story, but what made me love him then was his playfulness, his joy in making up stories and telling jokes, and his pipe. He kept his pipe with him, and the smell of pipe tobacco lingered on his clothing and imbued the living room with a smokey ambience. He filled his pipe bowl from a little tobacco sack with quick pinching motions, and the percussive rhythm of suck-puff-suck-puff as he set the fresh tobacco to light was mesmerizing. So although I was surprised to learn my grandfather had died, I immeiately understood it. I was told he had had cancer, and that made sense to me, given the smoking and the surgical scar across his chest. In the early 1970s, cancer had emerged from intentional obscurity as a central, tragic feature in films and on television.(1) A few years earlier (1969) the US had mandated cigarette packages labels stating the following: â€œWarning: The Surgeon General Has Determined That Cigarette Smoking Is Dangerous to Your Health.â€ (2) Popular depictions of cancer tended to be certain forms of cancer â€“ leukemia, brain tumors, lung cancer â€“ with differing references, and the patients depicted tended to be either elderly (and not the main character) or young, beautiful, and tragic. [examples] But even a child knew that an old man with a pipe habit was at risk for smoking-related cancer.

A procession of family deaths followed my grandfather's: his younger brother, my maternal grandfather, my great aunt, my maternal grandmother, and much later, my paternal grandmother. My father was diagnosed with various cancers he only grudgingly admitted to: bladder, elevated prostate, etc.; his cousin, my first cousin once removed, was diagnosed with early stage breast cancer in her early 60s. The family joked, and wondered, whether the flouroscope my paternal great-grandfather allowed the younger generation to play around might have had late-blooming ill effects. I recorded this grim family history in medical intake forms, but it wasn't until my own breast cancer diagnosis at age 47 that I learned that my Ashkenazi Jewish grandfather had died not of lung cancer, but of cancer of the breast.

Cancer's obscurity is rooted in multiple biological, social, and cultural pots. [biological: variety, knitting together through cell biology], social/demographic [age at death, rise of cancer], cultural [war on cancer, scientism, }

FN1: Love Story aired in 1970, but the leukemia of the book version was an undefined blood disease in the film. FN2: Surgeon General's report was dated 1964, so it took 5 years to get the labels out.

      An intellectual history of the concept of "stress"

      Midway through, Hogness ties the "systems theory" developments associated with WWII (and, presumably, the rise of the Military Industrial Complex ideology, although perhaps predating it)- think Weiner & cybernetics, but others as well - with the preceding medical research on the physiology of "stress" carried out by Selye.

      14: incapacity of soldiers themselves - battle fatigue. (Hogness argues that in the Civil War, soldiers who were incompetent in battle were judged as moral failures and "cowards"; in WWI, the concept was "shell shock" with imagined damage from percussive experiences; and in WWII, battle fatigue drew together a number of scientizing concepts and approaches. By the time of the Korean War, soldiers were being tested (blood draws, hormone levels) to learn about and diagnose stress in battle. "With the organizing idea of stress, medicine had an object of study, the endocrine system, that was more accessible than the nervous system had been. Physiologists joined psychologists and psychiatrists in the effort to understand soldiers incapacitated by the situation of war. Stress became a topic of military medical and psychiatric research." 14 On page 15, Hogness notes a symposium on stress and the military from 1953 â€“ it'd be fantastic to get that archive!

        Thoughts on Culture and Structure

        24th May 2022 at 10:53am

        Sahlins: Culture as its own force - what is its relationship to material forces? Marx: Culture: Does "culture" have its own existence outside of ideology? Raymond Williams: Culture as "keywords" - material culture gives rise to "culture"? Structure as fundamental; culture as dressing?

        Jessica and I are trying to read Sahlins â€“ we started because Jessica was writing a piece in dialog with Raymond Williams (country and city) and we began just talking about what was interesting about Williams' perspective; then there was a celebration of Sahlins' life and works at Chicago (he died in April 2021). I just read a wikipedia write-up of Sahlins' intellectual agenda. (Yes, wikipedia.) His first works were extensions of Leslie White's evolutionary theories, but arguing against biological determinism. Apparently he asserted that societies "evolve" into increasingly complex configurations to better "fit" their circumstances, and that "diffusion" also is part of the process of cultural change/evolution. He turned to a critique of neo-classical economics (one of his early colleagues - or was he a mentor? â€“ was Karl Polanyi) arguing that the cultural foundations of a society will shape/frame the "economic" behaviors, rather than the other way around. Anyway, we read an article, and will follow up with reading (re-reading) Culture and Practical Reason. But talking together, I felt clearer about the issue of anthropology having abandoned a central set of questions: essentially, the set of questions around how social structure and culture fit together. I don't think it's a simple question of one being primary, and the other secondary. Rather obviously, there's a dynamic relationship, and in some "basic" way maybe one is the driver, but maybe the most important mission of anthropology was to investigate how culture influences structure: how culture is not simply "clothing" or ideology, but is a framing force of the structures and dynamics of societies.

        In my own writing maybe my mission is to figure out how "the structure of the conjuncture," say, is apparent in the ways things get worked out in South Korea.
